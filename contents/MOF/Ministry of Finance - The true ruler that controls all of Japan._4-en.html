VII. Analysis: Just How Powerful Is the Ministry?

A. The Gap Between Constitutional Rules and Reality

As we have seen, the Constitution says sovereignty resides with the people, that elected Diet members pass laws and budgets, and that the Cabinet acts accordingly. Yet in reality unelected finance bureaucrats—armed with expertise, information, and vast authority over budgets and taxes—appear to wield huge influence over decision making.

They spread the fiscal-collapse narrative, push austerity such as consumption tax hikes, mold public opinion through the media, and use the National Tax Agency's investigative power to pressure opponents. Such behind-the-scenes exertion of power suggests a large gap between democratic ideals and political reality.

B. "If in Doubt, Explain!"—Is the Ministry Accountable?

In a democracy, those with power must fully explain how they use it—that is, provide accountability. When something seems suspicious or harmful to the public, information should be disclosed and explained in plain terms.

Yet the ministry's actions often fall short. It continues to provide misleading information about the managed currency system, gives unconvincing reasons for consumption tax hikes, and sidesteps questions about amakudari and media influence. In democracy the rule should be "if in doubt, fulfill your duty to explain," not "benefit of the doubt."

C. Are These Policies Really About Money?—Japan's Finances Viewed Through the Managed Currency System

Reexamining Japan's fiscal and economic policy through the lens of the managed currency system suggests its advantages are largely unused. Japan, which issues its own currency, cannot in principle default on yen-denominated debt. Nonetheless, citing debt and lack of resources, spending vital for citizens has been curtailed and growth opportunities lost.

To escape deflation, the government should spend more, yet the ministry keeps applying the brakes. It seems to intentionally hide the potential of the managed currency system and bind the public and politicians with the mantra of "no funding"—as if owning a well with limitless water but constantly insisting on water rationing.

D. What Drives the Ministry?—"Ministerial Interests" and a Misguided Sense of Justice

Why would the ministry promote policies that could hurt citizens and stall the economy? One motive is maximizing ministerial interests—the instinct to preserve and expand its authority. It fiercely resists transferring its budget and tax powers to others.

Another motive may be a distorted elite belief that "fiscal discipline is the highest virtue." Reducing government debt and improving fiscal balances become ends in themselves, relegating citizens' lives and the country's future to second place. This arrogance—that only the ministry truly understands public finance—may be steering Japan in the wrong direction.

Summary of This Section

  • A significant gap may exist between constitutional ideals and the ministry's real political clout.
  • The ministry is criticized for failing to fully explain its decision making and use of influence.
  • Many policies neglect the advantages of the managed currency system and choose austerity by claiming "no resources."
  • The ministry's actions seem driven by maximizing its own interests and by a belief that fiscal discipline is an unquestionable good.

VIII. Conclusion: Escaping the Ministry's Grip and Restoring Real Democracy

A. Summary—Is the Ministry Truly the Power Holder?

From various angles, this report suggests that the constitutional ideals of popular sovereignty and political leadership are being distorted by the ministry's enormous power and its austerity ideology—dubbed the "Zaimu Sect".

Beyond its legal powers over budgets, taxes, and tax investigations, the ministry monopolizes and distorts information about the managed currency system, controls the media, and influences personnel across government, effectively reigning as an absolute authority that drives flawed policies and weakens the nation.

B. What Happens If Nothing Changes?—Grave Effects on Democracy and the Economy

If unelected bureaucrats, especially finance officials, decide crucial policies without clear accountability, democracy itself is imperiled. As long as this warped structure persists, full escape from deflation is unlikely, growth will be stifled, inequality will widen, and the country will lose vitality. Policy will serve ministerial interests and a privileged few, not the public good.

C. How to Rebuild Japan—Dismantling the Ministry and Awakening the People

To break this seemingly hopeless situation and restore genuine sovereignty and prosperity, we need bold, fundamental reform—nothing less than a thorough overhaul of the ministry.

Proposals include:

  • Drastically reduce or dismantle the ministry's authority: Move budget authority to the Cabinet Office or a new powerful Diet bureau. Curtail the Tax Bureau's powers, place the National Tax Agency under an independent revenue service, and sharply cut the ministry's staff and budget.
  • Spread accurate understanding of the managed currency system: The government and Bank of Japan must clearly explain that yen-denominated bonds do not default and that bond issuance is new currency creation. This should be taught in schools and adult education.
  • Re-educate politicians and bureaucrats: Require all Diet members and senior officials to undergo intensive training on the managed currency system and macroeconomic management to free them from the ministry's "fiscal balance" ideology.
  • Normalize the media and ensure full disclosure: Abolish the press-club system and dismantle cozy ties between media and power. Fully disclose policy-making processes—including those of the ministry—to enable public oversight. Media must stop repeating ministry propaganda and report accurately on the managed currency system.
  • Create a truly independent economic advisory body: Establish an institution completely independent of the government to provide objective analysis and policy proposals that can counter the ministry's narrative.

D. The Future Is in Our Hands—Toward Liberation from the Ministry's "Spell"

For Japan to overcome decades of stagnation and allow all citizens to feel hopeful and prosperous, it must break free from the grip of this "invisible absolute authority". That path will not be easy. But each of us, as sovereign citizens, must think seriously about the situation, seek accurate information, raise our voices, and take action—there is no other way.

We hope this report offers a small step in that direction and helps you reflect on Japan's future.

Summary of This Section

  • Evidence suggests the ministry effectively rules Japan and has driven harmful policies.
  • If unchanged, democracy will hollow out, the economy will worsen, and living standards will decline.
  • Fundamental reforms—reducing the ministry's power, spreading correct knowledge of the managed currency system, re-educating officials, normalizing the media, and establishing independent analysis—are essential.
  • Ultimately, citizens recognizing the problem and taking action is the only path to break the ministry's spell.
